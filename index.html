The problem

The information exists.
You just can't get to it.

What you need to take a medicine safely is scattered across three documents, and at every handoff something breaks down. The richest guidance stays locked away, and what reaches you is hard to read, easy to lose, and impossible to adapt.

01 / The label

Built for the printer, not your eyes

Small type, all capitals, packed tight. No rule governs how a label looks, so legibility is left to chance, and dosage gets misread.

02 / The leaflet

Plain language you can't search

The Patient Medication Information sheet is plain enough, but it is a piece of paper. Easy to lose, impossible to search, and the same size for everyone.

03 / The monograph

Safety facts written for clinicians

Interactions, food timing, what to do about a side effect. The most useful answers live in a dense document meant for professionals.

Adverse drug events remain one of the most common, and most preventable, harms in health care.

Plain answers

The questions a label
never answers.

  • Can I take this with food?
  • What do I do if I miss a dose?
  • Does this interact with my other medications?
  • What are the serious side effects, and which need a doctor?
  • Is it safe to have a drink while I'm on this?
  • How should I store it, and when does it expire?

Every answer is drawn from the approved product information for your exact medicine. Not the open web.

One source of truth

Straight from the
approved monograph.

Health Canada already requires structured, plain-language patient information for every drug, and is moving to a machine-readable product monograph. Plainscript reads that authoritative, regulator-approved content and delivers it to the patient. The same source your pharmacist dispenses from, now legible. No new regulation. No bigger labels.
